\chapter{Evaluation: Intent-Aware Path Selection}
\label{ch:p1eval}

This chapter evaluates the Part~I selectors. It states the experimental setup and metrics
(\cref{sec:p1eval:setup}), then reports results against the six baselines
(\cref{sec:p1eval:baselines}), the intent-alignment behavior of the conditional agent
(\cref{sec:p1eval:intent}), the probing-overhead reduction (\cref{sec:p1eval:overhead}), and an ablation
ladder isolating each design element (\cref{sec:p1eval:ablation}). Several results tables depend on
experiment runs that are still in progress; those are marked and left as structured placeholders so that
the remaining experimental work is well defined.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:p1eval:setup}

\paragraph{Environments.} Experiments use BRITE-generated SCION topologies of varying size, each driven
by the 28-day hourly traffic model with per-link aggregation (\cref{sec:p1design:env}). Training and
evaluation are separated by holding out a disjoint set of source--destination pairs and hours, so that
reported numbers reflect generalization rather than memorization.
\gap{state the exact topology sizes, number of ASes/links, number of pairs, and train/test split
actually used, once the full pipeline run completes.}

\paragraph{Intents.} The conditional agent is trained across the reward profiles of
\cref{sec:p1design:film} (balanced, throughput, trust/loss-averse, latency, probe-frugal) and evaluated on
those profiles plus held-out interpolated intents to test generalization across the intent space.

\paragraph{Metrics.} For each method we report: the intent-weighted reward of \cref{eq:p1reward}; the
achieved per-flow latency and bandwidth; the probing overhead (in normalized probe cost); and the
per-decision selection time. Intent alignment is assessed both by reward under each intent and by
\emph{behavioral divergence} -- how much the chosen paths change as the intent changes.

\section{Intent Alignment}
\label{sec:p1eval:intent}

The defining test of the design is whether one conditioned policy actually \emph{changes its decisions}
with intent. We evaluate the same trained conditional agent under each intent on a fixed set of
pairs/hours and measure (i) reward under the matching intent versus reward under a mismatched intent, and
(ii) how the distribution of chosen paths shifts -- e.g.\ toward low-latency paths under a latency intent,
toward high-bandwidth paths under a throughput intent.

\section{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:p1eval:ablation}

We isolate the contribution of each design element by walking up the agent ladder of
\cref{sec:p1impl:agents}:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Flat vs.\ scoring.} Does the permutation-equivariant scoring architecture improve over a
  fixed-action DQN, and does it generalize across path counts that a flat agent cannot even represent?
  \item \textbf{Scoring vs.\ conditional.} Does adding intent conditioning let one model match a suite of
  per-intent-trained scoring models, avoiding per-objective retraining?
  \item \textbf{FiLM vs.\ concatenation.} The critical ablation: replace the FiLM head with plain
  concatenation of the intent vector and confirm the failure predicted in \cref{sec:p1design:concat} --
  that behavior becomes (near-)invariant to intent even though reward on the training intent may look
  acceptable.
\end{enumerate}
